What is GNU?

"I consider that the Golden Rule requires that if I like a program I must share it with other people who like it." -Richard Stallman

GNU is often described as a collection of free software. Though what makes GNU unique is the fact that it is driven by a philosophy of a free software culture which is described in The GNU Manifesto. GNU stands for GNU is Not Unix. Though the name can be confusing, it simply means that GNU software is a not a Unix derivative.

The GNU Manifesto was written by Richard Stallman in 1985, and it remains a thought-provoking document in the world of software development, culture, and business.

One of the compelling themes in the manifesto is the concept of “social contribution". Stallman argues that providing free and useful software to humanity is more important than simply making money. He makes the point that one should be able to build on, and edit, other people's work, so that humanity can collaboratively build a knowledge base.

Ultimately the question that GNU raises is “what kind of computer software culture do you want?” One that promotes sharing, remixing, and freedom of access, or one that is more interested in producing controlled commercial products?

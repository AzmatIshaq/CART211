What is GNU?

GNU is often described as a collection of free software. GNU stands for GNU is Not Unix. Though the name can be confusing, it simply means that GNU software is a not a Unix derivative.

What makes GNU unique though is not the name but its embodiment of the free software culture described by Richard Stallman in The GNU Manifesto. The manifesto was written in 1985, and it remains a thought-provoking document in the world of software development, culture, and business.

One of the compelling themes in the manifesto is the concept of “social contribution". Stallman argues that providing free and useful software to people is a moral obligation. He further makes the point that one should be able to build on, and edit, other developpers' work so that people can use software collaboratively for the betterment of humanity.

Ultimately the question that GNU raises is “what kind of computer software culture do we want?” One that promotes sharing, remixing, and freedom of access, or one that is more interested in producing controlled commercial products?



"I consider that the Golden Rule requires that if I like a program I must share it with other people who like it." -Richard Stallman
